Charting the Heavens

Tales of the Night Sky

A Journey Through the Sky

Have you ever looked up at the night sky, and wondered what you’re looking at? To me, it is simply amazing to think that we are looking at the same sky that our ancestors did. So here, we will learn about the 88 constellations in the sky and the heroes, creatures and myths connecting them all.

Constellations might look like nice patterns drawn across the sky, but actually they are not real groupings of stars. In fact, the stars in a constellation can be separated by vast, vast distances, often hundreds of thousands of lightyears. They only appear close together from our viewpoint on Earth as we cannot tell the difference in distance when we see them in the night sky. Regardless, for thousands of years, people from all over the world have connected these distant points of light into figures, turning the night sky into a world of imagination.

You might wonder, why is it that today, in a world where myths are receding and being replaced by science, that we still use constellations? This is because constellations now help astronomers to map out the sky. In fact, in 1922, the International Astronomical Union formally defined 88 constellations, which mapped regions that covered the entire sky. 48 of these constellations are based in ancient Greek mythology while the rest are later European additions. Together, they divide the entire celestial sphere into separate regions.

Origins & Navigation

In a time before maps, compasses, or GPS, the night sky was the first navigational guide people could rely on. By watching the steady movement of constellations, our ancestors learned to navigate vast distances and track the seasons.

Claudius Ptolemy

C. 100-160/170s AD

Claudius Ptolemy was a Greco-Roman mathematician, geographer and astronomer. He is best known for developing the geocentric model of the universe, with the Earth at its center, in his most famous work, the Almagest.

Additionally, the Almagest featured a set of 48 classical star groupings which even now we use as constellations. However, his catalogue of stars only included the visible stars which could be seen in the northern hemisphere, meaning that around half of the stars in the night sky weren’t included.

Fun fact: Ptolemy claimed to make a series of observations at noon everyday, but modern scientists have consistently found that the observations were half an hour too early!

Figures of Greek Myth

Before we explore the stars, here are some of the key figures from Greek mythology whose stories and creations are scattered across the heavens.

Zeus

Son of Cronus, Ruler of Mount Olympus, Father of Gods and Men, Zeus (or Jupiter/Jove according to the Romans) was the main deity that the ancient Greeks worshipped, and he placed many heroes and creatures into the skies.

Hera

Hera, or Juno, is the wife of Zeus. She is often portrayed in ancient texts as extremely jealous of her husband’s lovers and illegitimate children. Therefore, she often sent beasts into the sky, leaving her mark among the constellations.

Heracles

Heracles the lion-hearted, the mightiest Greek hero, son of Zeus, after carrying out twelve impossible tasks (The twelve labours of Heracles), had his mortal half burned away and ascended mount Olympus to reach godhood.

Poseidon

Known as Neptune to the Romans, he is the god of the seas. He is therefore thought to be connected to the sky’s water constellations, including some sea creatures.
